INTRODUCION

In Vietnam, kids learn programming in school at age 10.

In Estonia, that age drops down to 6.

In England, that age is 5.


With a spotlight cast on the Computer Science curriculum worldwide, there's been recent craze around the idea that "Coding is the new literacy."

No matter how many times you hear it, a job in Computer Science does not guarantee you financial success, or never-ending happiness for that matter.

However, there are still some compelling arguments to be made about why everyone should learn to code.

So, I will discuss the arguments of both sides to let readers That's you guys! formulate their own opinions. Then, to conclude, I will offer my personal take on why coding just isn't for everyone. Ready? Let's start!

The Skeptics

On one side of the argument, skeptics such as Ronny Scherer an education researcher at the University of Oslo and Peter J. Denning a computer scientist since the 1960‚Äôs who pioneered modern computer memory management are calling for the need for more empirical evidence suggesting that proficiency in programming develops proficiency in problem solving.

This "transfer of skill" from programming to problem solving is known as the Transfer Effect, and it's existence is the main contention of debate amongst the scholars.

Scherer casts doubt on the existence of such transfer effects, stating that the proponents‚Äô ‚Äúclaims have been supplemented with evidence from experimental studies to a very limited extent‚Äù and that ‚Äúmost of the empirical research on the transfer effects dates back to the 1980s and 1990s" (Scherer, Learning from the Past). Tagging along, Denning asserts that ‚Äúmany education researchers have searched for supporting evidence [of computer science transfer effects] but have not found any‚Äú (Denning). Citation 2

The Proponents

On the other side of the argument, proponents such as √ñzkan Vatanserver a teacher at The Ministry of National Education in Turkey and Ronny Scherer again, but this time as a proponent! point to their own empirical studies showing statistically significant links between learning coding and transfer skills to problem solving, creative skills, and mathematical skills (Scherer, The Cognitive Benefits of Learning Computer Programming Citation 4 ; Vatansever). Citation 3

Vatanserver's study consists of pre-trial and post-trial, qualitative and quantitative tests done on 5th and 6th graders learning to make games with Scratch https://scratch.mit.edu/ , a kid-friendly drag-and-drop programming environment.

As expected, this study done in September 2018 found a statistically significant link between learning coding and problem solving skills.

Scherer, despite publishing a journal calling the current empirical studies outdated, preformed his own study in which he aggregated as many relevant studies done on the transfer effects of programming as his team could find.

This study, published in July 2019, also found a statistically significant link, supporting the existence of programming transfer effects.

By presenting both individual and aggregate studies, the proponents combat the skeptics‚Äô argument of a lack of empirical evidence.

Therefore, as a counter-interpretation of the skeptical standpoint mentioned earlier, Proponents could interpret the abundance of empirical evidence as an indication that learning programming ‚Äî and thus computational thinking ‚Äî yields significant benefits on everyday problem solving:

Did They Just... Contradict Each Other?

That's right. Both the Skeptics and the Proponents believe they have the evidence on their side.

However, the key difference is the focus on time.

The Skeptical side relies on older data spanning a longer time window, while the Proponents' side relies on newer data spanning a smaller time window.

The question is, then, do you trust older, more consistent evidence, or you do trust newer, more prone to be disproved evidence?

In business, knowing how to code can open your chances of landing a high-paying job in the midst of a surge of demand for coders.

"In fact, even as the Department of Labor predicts the nation will add 1.2 million new computer-science-related jobs by 2022, we‚Äôre graduating proportionately fewer computer science majors than we did in the 1980s, and the number of students signing up for Advanced Placement computer science has flatlined."

Learning to code doesn't mean you have to quit your current occupation. In fact, it can complement it.

Knowing how to code also opens up your options to a nice, work-from-home freelance side gig.

For the entrepreneur types out there, knowing how to code can cut costs for any budding businesses. Instead of spending over let's say $200,000 USD in wage costs every year for workers to develop and maintain your business's website, what if you did everything yourself? Like any other job, coding isn't a free ticket to success.

Even if you graduated with a degree in computer science, there are roadblocks everywhere:

  • Clueless HR: Sometimes job requirements require 5 years of experience with a programming language that's only been out for 1!

  • Code Tests 1: Recruiters test you on impractical code skills for the job.
    (like reversing a linked-list or a binary tree) Both tests are extremely common in code interviews yet not indicative of practical skills for the job!

  • Code Tests 2: Some recruiters make you physically write your code on whiteboards

    The ancient tablets

  • Connections: If you don't have many connections to people already in the trade, your chances just plummeted.

  • Volatile Changes: You might spend a whole year learning a programming language and have to forget it the next. Tech changes quick, and so do programming conventions. For the busy parent or the overloaded college student: it's a matter of life or livelihood.

Let's say you made it past these roadblocks and got for your first job as a coder. What awaits you then?

You've spent years coding out of your pure love for craft. You've finished countless personal projects, and now you're ready to tackle some real work.

However, as you find out quickly, things are different when you code for work from when you code for fun.

Now, you're working 9-to-5 for someone else's goals, making money but not the satisfaction ‚Äî the life that once sprung from your own two hands.



Programming is no longer a hobby.

Programming is work.

What was once fun is now work.
